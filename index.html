001 / About

A practitioner working at the seam between AI security and workforce development.

My research and practice sit at the intersection of three accelerating shifts: the rapid integration of generative AI into critical infrastructure, the corresponding emergence of new attack surfaces (prompt injection, agentic exploits, model supply chain risks), and the structural lag in how the cybersecurity workforce is trained to meet them.

I work across frameworks — NICE for workforce mapping, OWASP for threat taxonomies, MITRE for adversary modeling — to design educational interventions that translate cutting-edge security research into something practitioners and students can actually act on. My current focus is the GenAI threat surface and how organizations build the human capacity to manage it.

I'm based in Washington, D.C., and I'm currently developing this work into a doctoral research agenda focused on AI security and cybersecurity education.

002 / Focus areas

Four threads, one through-line.

→ 01

GenAI threat modeling

Adversarial techniques against language models and agentic systems — prompt injection, jailbreaks, indirect injection, model exfiltration — and what defending them looks like in practice.

→ 02

The NICE Framework

Mapping evolving cybersecurity work roles, knowledge, skills, and tasks to actual training pathways — and arguing for new ones the framework hasn't yet captured.

→ 03

OWASP contribution

Active contributor to the Agentic AI Top 10 and adjacent OWASP workstreams. Translating community-driven threat research into educational content for the next generation of defenders.

→ 04

The Curriculum Patch

A framework for layering current threat content onto existing cybersecurity courses without waiting for textbooks to catch up — closing the gap between threat research and the classroom.

→ 05

Cloud security education

Building cloud-native, scenario-based labs that mirror the environments graduates actually walk into — not the abstract networks of legacy curricula.

→ 06

International cooperation

Cybersecurity workforce development across the Francophone world, with particular interest in how frameworks like NICE adapt to national strategies and capacity-building goals.

The NICE Conference convenes the cybersecurity education and workforce community in Philadelphia each year. My session focuses on how K-12 and early-career cybersecurity programs must evolve to meet a threat landscape now defined by cloud-native infrastructure and generative AI.

NICE Conference / 2026

Future-Ready K-12 Cybersecurity: Cloud, GenAI, and the Curriculum Patch

Session · 50 min Practitioner Track

A session on closing the lag between the cybersecurity threat landscape and the curricula meant to prepare people for it. I introduce the Curriculum Patch framework — a method for layering current threat content onto existing courses — and walk through two student-facing artifacts I've developed: a structured Red Team Model for GenAI and the Bank Heist Lab, an incident response simulation built around a modern, AI-augmented attack chain.
